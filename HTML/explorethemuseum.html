About the Museum

Here is your gateway to an enriching journey through history and culture. Whether you're a seasoned museum-goer or a curious first-timer, this page offers insights from others who have visited these places, sharing the treasures and stories housed within our island.

"For having worked 5 years here i am always amazed by the beauty of the treasures that contain blue penny. From the statue of Paul et virginie and rare collection of stamps and other things if you love to venture in past history, this place is made for you. One thing I can tell you that not everybody knows is that the name of the museum comes from a stamp that doesn't exist, which is actually quite funny. You see, the Blue Penny stamp is actually a two pence stamp, while the Red Penny stamp is the one penny. So, in essence, we work in a museum named after a stamp that doesn't exist! It’s a quirky little detail that adds to the charm of the Blue Penny Museum."

The Line Barracks in Port Louis, Mauritius, have a rich and significant history. Established in 1740 during the French colonial period, the Line Barracks were originally built to house French soldiers. This military complex has played a crucial role in the island’s defense and administration throughout its history. During the British colonial period, which began in 1810, the barracks continued to serve as a military installation. The British expanded and fortified the site, and it became the headquarters for the British garrison in Mauritius. The complex included various buildings, such as officers’ quarters, stables, and storage facilities.

The National Assembly of Mauritius, formerly known as the Legislative Assembly, serves as the unicameral legislature of the country. Established under the Westminster system of parliamentary democracy, it plays a pivotal role in governing Mauritius. Comprising 70 members, 62 are directly elected for five-year terms from multi-member constituencies, while 8 additional members ensure equitable representation of ethnic and religious minorities.

The main shrine of the 'Kailasson' temple features intricate sculptures depicting various forms of Shiva and mythological stories. The artwork at the Sockalingam Meenakshi Amman temple is unique in Mauritius, inspired by the style of the Meenakshi temple in Madurai, India. It showcases Tamilian influences with expressive details and graceful poses. This craftsmanship is a rare gem in Mauritian culture, setting Kailasson apart from newer temples where such quality is harder to find.
