History

Port Louis has a rich and intriguing history that dates back to the 16th century. The city's initial settlers were the Dutch, who arrived in 1598 and established a trading post. The French followed, seizing control of the city in 1735. They named it Port Louis in honor of King Louis XV of France and made it the capital of Mauritius. During French rule, Port Louis thrived, becoming a significant port in the region. It served as the main port for the French East India Company and a major hub for the slave trade. The city was also home to many affluent French merchants and aristocrats, who constructed grand mansions and gardens. Over the years, Port Louis continued to grow and evolve. Today, it is a bustling city with a population of over 150,000 people, serving as the economic and political center of Mauritius and a melting pot of diverse cultures and religions.

Postal Museum

The postal service in Mauritius started in 1772 under French rule, using slaves to deliver letters. There were no dedicated postal buildings, just a delivery service. This system fell into disuse but was revived by the British in 1834, starting officially in January 1835, with inland letters delivered three times a week. The original Central Post Office opened in July 1836 on ‘La Chaussée,’ and the first postage stamps were issued in 1847. In 1958, the General Post Office was declared a national monument by Governor Sir Robert Scott. This status was reaffirmed in 1985 and listed as a national monument in the 2003 National Heritage Fund Act. It is an important heritage site within the Aapravasi Ghat World Heritage Buffer Zone and Port Louis.

Place D'armes

Place D’Armes is renowned as the heart of Port Louis, majestically lined with tall palm trees and surrounded by old cannons partially buried in the ground. During the early French occupation, Governor De Nyon established a fortification and battery here, which were later reinforced by Mahe de La Bourdonnais. This fortification was known as ‘La Batterie du Salut’. Place D’Armes quickly became a favorite meeting spot for various activities, from gossiping and critiquing to admiring ships entering and leaving the port. It also became famous for its taxi stand, evolving from animal-driven carriages to motorized vehicles, a legacy that continues today. In the 1940s, an arched gate was erected at the entrance of Place D’Armes to honor Mahe de La Bourdonnais, the founder of Port Louis. In the late 1960s, a roundabout with a large fountain was added at this entrance, enhancing the charm of Port Louis for nearly two decades. Unfortunately, due to the poor maintenance made, the fountain had to be discarded the early 1980s.

Citadel Fort Adelaide

Fort Adelaide, completed in 1840, was originally constructed to protect the harbor from invasions and suppress local uprisings after the racial tensions of the era. Although intended for military purposes, it was never used as such since tensions had subsided by its completion. The Citadel of Port Louis remains a significant symbol of the transitional period between the abolition of slavery and the arrival of indentured laborers, reflecting the shift from slavery to Indian immigration. In the 1830s, under British rule, Mauritius faced strong opposition from French settlers due to the loss of the island and the abolition of slavery.
The fort was approved for construction to defend against potential invasions or rebellions, strategically located on a hill to control the city, port, and surrounding areas. Elements of an earlier fortification from 1743 were incorporated into its structure. By 1840, the situation had improved: France no longer sought the island’s sugar plantations, and French settlers received compensation for the abolition of slavery, improving their relations with local authorities. Consequently, the fort's military purpose diminished, and it was used for signaling and ceremonial purposes. Over time, Fort Adelaide became a cultural venue, hosting concerts, shows, and events. Today, it offers guided tours, a museum, a restaurant, an art gallery, and shops, serving as a place of relaxation and discovery.

Aapravasi Ghat

The Aapravasi Ghat is a crucial symbol of Mauritian identity, serving as the entry point for the ancestors of over 70% of today’s Mauritian population. The abolition of slavery by the British Parliament in 1833 led to the establishment of the indenture system. Indentured laborers were free men and women who signed contracts to work abroad for a specified period, usually five years, with terms covering wages, working hours, type of work, rations, housing, and medical care. Before the official abolition of slavery on 1 February 1835, Mauritian planters were already seeking laborers to meet the growing demands of the sugar industry. The British colonial government launched the "Great Experiment" in Mauritius to evaluate and demonstrate the superiority of free over slave labor. This experiment began on 2 November 1834 with the arrival of the ship Atlas from India, carrying 36 indentured laborers.
Immigrants typically arrived in Mauritius after an eight to ten-week journey from India. At Aapravasi Ghat, they received food, shelter, and medical care if needed. Upon arrival, a medical officer inspected them and vaccinated them for smallpox if necessary. Those with contagious diseases were sent to quarantine stations on small islets around Mauritius, while healthy immigrants disembarked and were registered in the depot yard. Sick individuals were sent to the civil hospital. The Immigration Depot staff recorded each immigrant's personal details. In 1865, a photographic unit was established, and a photographer took two portrait photos of each immigrant—one for the immigrant’s ticket and the other for the records office at Aapravasi Ghat. These documents are now preserved at the Mahatma Gandhi Institute.

Jummah Masjid

The Jummah Mosque, located on Royal Street in Port Louis, opened in 1853 and has since become the most prominent mosque in the city. The mosque's significance is such that the priest in charge is bestowed the title of Pesh Imam (equivalent to high priest). Between 1878 and 1895, various expansion and enhancement works were undertaken to accommodate the growing needs of the Muslim community. The construction of the mosque at the corner of Royal and Queen Streets was initiated to meet the religious needs of Muslim traders in Port Louis, sparing them the long and tiring journey to the Al-Aqsa Mosque, the first Asian prayer house of Mauritius, located in the eastern suburb of Plaine Verte. The Jummah Mosque is renowned for its live broadcasts of Jummah prayers every Friday. It also broadcasts taraweeh prayers during Ramadan, Eid prayers, and various spiritual programs. These include Majilis during the first ten days of Muharram, the first twelve days of Rabi ul Awwal, Mehfil e Ashurah, and numerous ‘Urs Shareef celebrations held throughout the year.
